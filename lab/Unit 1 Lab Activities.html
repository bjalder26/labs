    9. Select water.
    10. Click on Liquid.
    11. Change the thermometer at top to read in degrees Celsius (°C) instead of Kelvin (K).
    12. Cool the water to 4°C by dragging the slider at the bottom down. If you go below 4°C, then drag the slider up until you reach 4°C.
    13. Notice how densly packed together the water molecules are.
    14. Now cool the water to below 0°C (the freezing point) by pulling the slider down again.
    15. Notice how densely packed together the water molecules are now compared to when the water was a liquid (at 4°C).
    16. Is water more dense as a liquid or a solid?
    17. Notice how in the solid state (ice), the water molecules tend to stay oriented in such a way so that the oxygen atoms (red) in one molecule is near the hydrogen atoms (white) in another molecule. This maximizes hydrogen bonding, which is a lower energy state. Maximizing hydrogen bonding is what causes ice to float on water.
    18. An interesting fact is that water reaches its maximum density at about 4°C. As it cools below 4°C it starts maximizing the number of hydrogen bonds and becoming less dense. At 0°C water will fully maximize its hydrogen bonds froming a crystaline structure that will float on water. This creates an interesting temperature stratification in lakes during the winter where the coldest water is near the frozen surface and the warmest water is at the bottom of the lake. The top (least dense) layer of ice forms an insulating barrier and the warmest water (most dense at about 4°C) sinks to the bottom away from the cold air. That prevents the entire water body from freezing solid, allowing aquatic life to survive below.
