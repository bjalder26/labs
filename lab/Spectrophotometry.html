Equations and Calculations:

  • Y = mX + b
    • m = slope, b = y-intercept
  • A = εbC
    • A = absorbance, ε = molar absorptivity (molar extinction coefficient), b = path length, C = concentration (molarity)
  • c = λν
    • c = 3.00 x 108m/s, λ = wavelength, ν = frequency

Safety:

  • Potassium permanganate is a strong oxidizing agent and can cause skin and eye irritation. Avoid direct contact with the skin or eyes. If contact occurs, rinse the affected area immediately with water.
  • Wear safety goggles or glasses at all times.
  • Wear nitrile gloves at all times.

Disposal:

  • Dilute to a very light pink color before disposing down the drain.

    14. Part D: Flame test.

    A flame test can be used to identify a compound based on its emission spectra. The emission spectra is the colors emitted by a hot substance. This is the opposite of a absorption spectra in which continous light is passed through a cold material to see what colors of light it absorbs.

    When a substance is heated its electrons become excited, and when those electrons fall back down to ground state they will release energy in the form of a photon. Since that photon has a specific amount of energy it will also have a specific frequency, wavelenght and color (Ephoton = hν).

    In multi-electron elements shielding causes each element to have slightly different energy electron shell levels, which leads to different colors of light being emitted. If we used a prisim to seperate the light, then we could accurately identify each element. However, we will be just observing the light emitted, and we will see a mixture of each color emitted. Because of this, some of the colors can be difficult to destingiush. You will be given 5 metal ions to identify based on their color. Use the table below, and the image below to help identify them. Keep in mind that simply disturbing the flow of your flame will cause an orange color. Try to distinguish this color from the color from each metal ion.
