Background: In this laboratory activity, you will be conducting various chemical reactions at different stations to observe and identify evidence of chemical changes. Chemical reactions involve the transformation of substances into new substances with different properties. During a chemical reaction, one or more reactants are converted into one or more products through the breaking and forming of chemical bonds. In order to determine if a chemical reaction has occurred, we look for certain indications or evidence, which include:
  • Color change: A noticeable change in color can be an indication that a chemical reaction has taken place. The appearance of a new color suggests the formation of different chemical species.
  • Gas formation: The evolution of gas bubbles or the production of gases during a reaction can be evidence of a chemical change. The formation of a gas may be observed as the release of bubbles or effervescence.
  • Solid formation (precipitation): The formation of a solid from the reaction of two solutions is called precipitation. It can be observed as the appearance of a cloudy or solid material within the solution.
  • Temperature change: A noticeable change in temperature, either an increase (exothermic reaction) or decrease (endothermic reaction), can indicate the occurrence of a chemical reaction.
  • New scent: The production of a new odor or the change in scent during a reaction can be an indication of a chemical change. Some reactions can produce characteristic or distinctive smells.
  • Formation of light or a flame: Some reactions involve the production of light or the presence of a flame, indicating the release of energy or the occurrence of a combustion reaction.
During this lab, you will visit different stations where specific chemical reactions will be performed. Your task is to carefully observe each reaction and record any evidence of a chemical change according to the provided instructions. These observations will be recorded in Table 3. Remember to follow all safety guidelines and instructions provided by your instructor. Safety goggles, gloves, and appropriate lab attire should be worn throughout the experiment. Any chemical spills or accidents should be reported to the instructor immediately. By conducting these experiments and recognizing the evidence of chemical reactions, you will develop a deeper understanding of the characteristics and indicators of chemical changes.

Safety:

  • Hydrochloric acid is corrosive and should be handled with care. If there is accidental contact with skin or eyes, rinse with copious amounts of water and seek medical attention if necessary.
  • Ammonium carbonate is a respiratory and eye irritant. Avoid contact with skin and eyes. In case of contact, rinse with water.
  • Hydrogen peroxide can cause skin and eye irritation.
  • Avoid inhalation of dust particles of potassium iodide. In case of contact with eyes or skin, rinse thoroughly with water.
  • Magnesium can react vigorously with certain substances and produce flammable hydrogen gas.
  • Ethyl alcohol is flammable and should be handled with caution.Use in a well-ventilated area away from open flames or sparks.
  • Copper (II) chloride can cause skin and eye irritation. In case of contact with skin or eyes, rinse thoroughly with water.
  • Wear lab goggles at all times.
  • Wear nitrile gloves at all times.

    Procedure:

  1. Obtain a test tube rack, and 6 test tubes.
  2. Label the test tubes 1 through 6.
  3. Visit all 3 stations and perform the chemical reactions at each station. They do not need to be visited in order.
  4. Watch for signs of chemical reactions and record them in table 3.

    5. Station 1 (in hood): Gas producing reaction and testing for gases

  5. Make sure the hood is on.
  6. In test tube 1, mix a plastic (Pasteur) pipet full of vinegar, with a pea size portion of sodium bicarbonate.
  7. Plug up the top of the top of the test tube with your gloved thumb to accumulate any gases that are forming.
  8. Light a wood splint, then immediately after removing your thumb from the test tube, put the splint into the test tube (but not in the liquid) and observe what happens. You may need to hold the flame and test tube outside of the hood briefly. Do not point the test tube toward anyone.
  9. Feel the bottom of the test tube for a temperature change.
  10. Record your observations in table 3.
  11. In test tube 2, mix a plastic (Pasteur) pipet full of hydrogen peroxide, with a pea size portion of potassium iodide.
  12. Plug up the top of the top of the test tube with your gloved thumb to accumulate any gases that are forming.
  13. Light a wood splint, but let the flame go down so that it is just “glowing”, then put it into the test tube (but not in the liquid) and observe what happens.
  14. Feel the bottom of the test tube for a temperature change.
  15. Record your observations in table 3.
  16. In test tube 3, mix a plastic (Pasteur) pipet full of 1.0 M hydrochloric acid, with a 1-cm piece of magnesium ribbon.
  17. Plug up the top of the top of the test tube with your gloved thumb to accumulate any gases that are forming.
  18. Light a wood splint, then put it into the test tube (but not in the liquid) and observe what happens.
  19. Feel the bottom of the test tube for a temperature change.
  20. Record your observations in table 3.
  21. In test tube 4, add a pea size portion of ammonium carbonate.
  22. Turn on the gas to the Bunsen burner, and carefully light it.
  23. Using a test tube holder, heat the ammonium carbonate over a flame and observe what happens.
  24. DO NOT PUT THE HOT TEST TUBE BACK IN YOUR TEST TUBE RACK.
  25. Using forceps, place a piece of red litmus paper by the mouth of test tube 4.
  26. Gently waft the air from the test tube to your nose to smell the gas. Reheat the sample and waft more vigerously if you can't smell anything. There should be a pungent odor.
  27. Turn off the Bunsen burner, and leave test tube 4 in the waste beaker located at station 1.
  28. Record your observations in table 3.

    29. Station 2: Precipitation reaction

  29. In test tube 5, mix a plastic (Pasteur) pipet full of copper (II) chloride, with a pea size portion of solid zinc.
  30. Note changes to both the liquid and the solid
  31. Record your observations in table 3.

    32. Station 3: Combustion reactions (in hood)

  32. Make sure the hood is on.
  33. Fill test tube 6 three quarters full with ice.
  34. Use a plastic (Pasteur) pipet to put enough ethanol into a evaporation dish or watch glass to cover the bottom.
  35. Carefully use a lighter to ignite the ethanol.
  36. Wipe off test tube 6, then use a test tube holder to hold test tube 6 above the flame, and observe the outside of the test tube for product formation.
  37. Let the ethanol completely burn out.
  38. Record your observations in table 3.
  39. Set up a watch glass near the Bunsen burner.
  40. Turn on the gas to the Bunsen burner, and light the burner.
  41. Use beaker tongs to hold a 2 cm piece of magnesium ribbon in the flame.
  42. DO NOT look directly at the magnesium as it burns, but when it starts to burn, move the magnesium over your watch glass.
  43. Turn off the gas to the Bunsen burner.
  44. When it is finished burning, observe the watch glass and record your observations for this reaction in table 3.
  45. Dispose of the waste in your remaining test tubes in the waste beaker.
  46. Wash your test tubes, watch glasses, and evaporation dish, then rinse them 3-7 times and leave them on paper towels to dry. Put everything else back where you found it.
  47. Clean up any messes and make sure the solution bottles are capped before you leave.

Measurement & Data Skill Building

Stoichiometry Problems Based on Lab Reactions

Answer the following questions about the reactions you performed using the given balanced equations. Each question refers to a specific test tube or reaction from the experiment.

  1. Test Tube 1 – Sodium bicarbonate
    Reaction from Test Tube 1:
    NaHCO3(s) + CH3COOH(aq) → CO2(g) + H2O(l) + CH3COONa(aq)

    A student used 0.84 g of sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3 in test tube 1.

    Calculate:
    a) the number of moles of sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3: mol

    b) the number of formula units of sodium bicarbonate used: formula units

  2. Test Tube 2 – Hydrogen peroxide
    Reaction from Test Tube 2:
    2 H2O2(aq) → 2 H2O(l) + O2(g)

    During the reaction, it is estimated that 3.0 × 1022 molecules of hydrogen peroxide, H2O2, decomposed.

    Calculate:
    a) the number of moles of hydrogen peroxide, H2O2: mol

    b) the mass in grams of hydrogen peroxide that reacted: g

  3. Test Tube 3 – Magnesium
    Reaction from Test Tube 3:
    Mg(s) + 2 HCl(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)

    A piece of magnesium metal, Mg, with a mass of 0.15 g was used.

    Calculate:
    a) the number of moles of magnesium, Mg: mol

    b) the number of atoms of magnesium that reacted: atoms

  4. Test Tube 4 – Ammonium carbonate
    Reaction from Test Tube 4:
    (NH4)2CO3(s) → 2 NH3(g) + CO2(g) + H2O(g)

    A student heated 1.25 g of ammonium carbonate, (NH4)2CO3.

    Calculate:
    a) the number of moles of ammonium carbonate, (NH4)2CO3: mol

    b) the number of formula units of ammonium carbonate decomposed: formula units

  5. Test Tube 5 – Zinc
    Reaction from Test Tube 5:
    Zn(s) + CuCl2(aq) → ZnCl2(aq) + Cu(s)

    After the reaction, 2.5 × 1021 atoms of zinc, Zn, were found to have reacted.

    Calculate:
    a) the number of moles of zinc, Zn: mol

    b) the mass in grams of zinc that reacted: g

  6. Ethanol combustion
    Reaction:
    C2H5OH(l) + 3 O2(g) → 2 CO2(g) + 3 H2O(g)

    During combustion, 0.92 g of ethanol, C2H5OH, was burned.

    Calculate:
    a) the number of moles of ethanol, C2H5OH: mol

    b) the number of molecules of ethanol burned: molecules

  7. Magnesium oxide – product from magnesium combustion
    Reaction:
    2 Mg(s) + O2(g) → 2 MgO(s)

    After burning magnesium, a student collected 0.40 g of magnesium oxide, MgO.

    Calculate:
    a) the number of moles of magnesium oxide, MgO: mol

    b) the number of formula units of magnesium oxide produced: formula units
