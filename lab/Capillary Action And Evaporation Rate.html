Part A: Capillary Action

Background:

Capillary action refers to the ability of a liquid to flow in narrow spaces without assistance of external forces and sometimes against external forces such as gravity. This phenomenon occurs due to the balance between two forces: cohesion (the attraction between molecules of the same substance) and adhesion (the attraction between molecules of different substances). When a liquid comes into contact with a solid surface, adhesion causes the liquid to spread along the surface, while cohesion holds the liquid molecules together. In a capillary tube, adhesion causes the liquid to climb up the walls, while cohesion pulls the rest of the liquid along with it. Different liquids exhibit varying degrees of capillary action depending on their adhesive and cohesive properties. Water and methanol have different molecular structures, which influence their capillary rise.
