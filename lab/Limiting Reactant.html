Limiting Reactant Lab

Name:

Read the prelab in Canvas. In this lab we will be learning about limiting reactants. Be certain to follow all instructions. Always properly dispose of broken glass and chemical waste according to your instructor.

Materials:

  • Copper(II) chloride dihydrate (CuCl2·2H2O)
  • Alumininum foil (Al)
  • Methanol
  • 6 M Hydrochloric acid (HCl)

Per each group:

  • 250 mL beakers (3)
  • Glass stirring rod
  • 20-50 mL graduated cylinder
  • 10 mL graduated cylinders (2)
  • Deionized water
  • Plastic pasteur pipets
  • Permenent marker or wax pencil

Equations and Calculations:

  • Molarity (M) = $\frac{mol_{solute}}{L{solution}}$
  • Molar mass = $\frac{grams}{mol}$
  • For aA → bB, Mole A x $\frac{b\;mol\;B}{a\;mol\;A}$ = Mol B
  • Percent yield = $\frac{Actual\;yield}{Theoretical\;yield}$ x 100%

Safety:

  • Goggles and nitril gloves must be worn at all times.
  • NaOH is a strong base and can cause burns. Avoid contact with body tissues.
  • Methanol is toxic if ingested or inhailed, and is very flamiable. Do not use methanol near open flames.
  • Concentrated hydrochloric acid is a strong acid and can damage body tissue. Avoid inhalation, ingestion, and contact body tissues. Store in a ventilation hood.
  • Waste should not go down the drain. Store in a waste container provided by your instructor.

Procedure:

  1. Label two 150 mL beakers "1" and "2". Record the mass of beaker 1 (you will not need the mass of beaker 2).
  2. Measure out the following amounts of CuCl2·2H2O, add it to the respective beaker, and record the actual masses.
    • Beaker 1: 0.50 g CuCl2·2H2O
    • Beaker 2: 0.70 g CuCl2·2H2O
  3. Add 10 mL deionized water to each of the beakers.
  4. Gently stir the solutions with a glass rod until the copper salt is completely dissolved.
  5. Measure out the following masses of aluminum foil and add the foil to the respective beakers in small pieces. Record the actual masses.
    • Beaker 1: 0.25 g Al(s)
    • Beaker 2: 0.05 g Al(s)
  6. Inspect the contents of each beaker and record your observations (e.g. color, small, bubbling, heat formation, etc.).
  7. Stir the contents of each beaker periodically with a glass stirring rod and record any changes you observe.
  8. If you completed the prelab, then you should know when the reactions are complete. Once the reactions are complete, record the colors of the beaker contents, how much solid Al remains, and any other observations.
  9. Set beaker 2 aside.
  10. In beaker 1, if excess aluminum foil remains, then in a vent hood, add 6 M HCl in small portions with constant stirring until the foil is completely reacted.
  11. Allow the solid copper to settle to the bottom, then use a plastic Pasteur pipet to suction off the solution being careful not to lose any of the copper.
  12. Wash the copper solid with 15 mL of deionized water, let the solid settle, and suction off the solution again. Repeat by washing and suctioning again.
  13. (note: this step washes away any dissolved ions)
  14. Wash the copper solid with 10 mL of methanol, let the solid settle, and suction off the solution.
  15. (note: this step washes away the water, which evaporates slowly, and replaces it with methanol which evaporates quickly)
  16. In a vent hood, heat beaker 1 containing the copper solid on a hot plate at a low setting ultil dry. Do not heat at high temperatures for longer periods of time, because this may cause oxidation of the copper.
  17. Cool beaker 1, then record the mass of beaker 1 and its contents.
  18. Label a third 150 mL beaker "3".
  19. Add 0.70 g CuCl2·2H2O (record actual mass) and 10 mL deionized water to beaker 3.
  20. Determine how much Al(s) is needed in grams (i.e. the stoichiometric amount) in order to completely react all of the CuCl2. Measure this amount out, record the mass, and add it to the beaker in small pieces.
  21. Record your observations before the reaction (initially), during the reaction, and at the end of the reaction.
  22. Dispose of the contents of the beakers in a container specified by your instructor.
  23. Complete the calculations in the analysis section.

Part D: Density of an Unknown Solid

  1. Select an unknown metal from the samples provided.
  2. Weigh the metal and record the mass.
  3. Use a ruler (or caliper) to measure the volume; (measure length, width, and height for a cube, or diameter and height for a cylinder - use the volume formula above to calculate volume). Record the volume.
    • Note: you may need to convert from mm to cm
  4. Calculate the density using the ruler (or caliper) method for volume and the displacement method for volume.
  5. Use a graduated cylinder to determine volume by water displacement. Record the volume to the nearest estimated 0.1 mL.
  6. Repeat steps 2-5 for the second trial.
  7. Take an average of the densities by water displacement for your two trials.
  8. Compare your average density to the densities in table 7 to determine the identity of your unknown solid.

Part E: Density of a Penny

  1. Collect 26 pennies from the pool provided.
  2. Weigh the pennies and record the mass.
  3. Fill a 50 mL graduated cylinder to 40.0 mL, then add the 26 pennies one-at-a-time
  4. Roll the pennies into the graduated cylinder to minimize splashing and bubbles getting trapped under the pennies.
  5. Periodically tap the side of the graduated cylinder to loosen up any trapped bubbles (bubbles will alter your volume measurement).
  6. Determine volume by water displacement (subtract the ending volume from the beginning volume of 40.0 mL). Record the volume to the nearest estimated 0.1 mL.
  7. Calculate the density of the pennies.
  8. Completely dry your pennies, then repeat steps 2-7 for trial 2.
  9. Calculate the average density from your two trials.
  10. Calculate percent composition of copper and zinc (using the formula at the beginning).
