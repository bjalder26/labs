Background

In this experiment, you will synthesize calcium carbonate (CaCO3) using a precipitation reaction, then determine how efficient your reaction was by calculating percent yield.

A precipitation reaction occurs when two aqueous (dissolved in water) ionic compounds react to form an insoluble solid, called a precipitate. In this lab, aqueous sodium carbonate (Na2CO3) reacts with aqueous calcium chloride (CaCl2) to form solid calcium carbonate:

$$ \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 (aq) + \text{CaCl}_2 (aq) \rightarrow 2\ \text{NaCl} (aq) + \text{CaCO}_3 (s) $$

In solution, these compounds dissociate into ions:

  • $$ \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 (aq) \rightarrow 2\ \text{Na}^+ (aq) + \text{CO}_3^{2-} (aq) $$
  • $$ \text{CaCl}_2 (aq) \rightarrow \text{Ca}^{2+} (aq) + 2\ \text{Cl}^- (aq) $$

The calcium ions and carbonate ions combine to form solid calcium carbonate:

$$ \text{Ca}^{2+} (aq) + \text{CO}_3^{2-} (aq) \rightarrow \text{CaCO}_3 (s) $$

Limiting Reactant and Theoretical Yield

In chemical reactions, the amount of product formed depends on the limiting reactant—the reactant that is completely used up first. In this lab, sodium carbonate is present in excess, so calcium chloride is the limiting reactant.

Using stoichiometry, you can calculate the theoretical yield, which is the maximum amount of calcium carbonate that could be formed if the reaction goes perfectly.

Actual Yield and Percent Yield

The amount of product you actually collect in the lab is called the actual yield. This is often less than the theoretical yield due to factors such as:

  • Loss of product during transfer or filtration
  • Incomplete reaction
  • Product remaining dissolved in solution

To evaluate the efficiency of your experiment, you will calculate percent yield:

$$ \text{Percent yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100\% $$

A percent yield close to 100% indicates a very efficient process, while lower values suggest sources of error or product loss.

Separation by Suction Filtration

After the reaction, the solid calcium carbonate must be separated from the liquid. This is done using suction filtration with a Büchner funnel. The vacuum pulls liquid through the filter paper, leaving the solid behind.

The solid is then dried to remove water, since any remaining moisture would increase the measured mass and lead to an inaccurate yield.

Real-World Connection

Calcium carbonate is a common compound found in chalk, limestone, and seashells. This lab models how chemists produce and isolate solid compounds from solution and evaluate the efficiency of chemical processes.

Equations and calculations:

  • Molarity (M) = $\frac{mol_{solute}}{L{solution}}$
  • Molar mass = $\frac{grams}{mol}$
  • For aA → bB, Mole A x $\frac{b\;mol\;B}{a\;mol\;A}$ = Mol B

Part A: Synthesize Calcium carbonate

You mix solutions of sodium carbonate and calcium chloride to produce calcium carbonate.
  1. Make sure the oven in the lab is set at 140°C, and turned on. It can take several minutes for the oven to heat to 140°C.
  2. Calculate what volume of 0.100 M calcium chloride needed to produce 1.00 g of calcium carbonate (100.09 g/mol) assuming an excess of sodium carbonate and record that amount below.

    1.00 g CaCO3 x $\frac{1\;mol\;CaCO_3}{100.09\;g\;CaCO_3}$ x $\frac{1\;mol\;CaCl_2}{1\;mol\;CaCO_3}$ x $\frac{1\;L\;CaCl_2}{0.100\;mol\;CaCl_2}$ x $\frac{1000\;mL}{1\;L}$ = mL CaCl2

  5. Plug in your hot plate and place the 400 mL beaker on the hot plate, then turn the hot plate up to a high setting.
  6. Observe the beaker string occasionally with the glass stirring rod. As a good practice, do not observe the beaker from above, and make sure your goggles are on. Record your observations below.
  7. Once the solution starts to boil (not a full boil), turn off the hot plate, unplug it, and move the solution to your lab bench using beaker tongs or heat resistant gloves.
  8. Allow the solution to cool for 15 minutes.

  20. Once water stops dripping from the funnel, turn off the vacuum.
