Background:

  • Acid-base reactions are exothermic reactions that produce water molecules, and a salt. Salts are ionic compounds that dissolve in water, so there won’t be any visual confirmation of an acid-base reaction. However, a clue that an acid base reaction occurred is that heat is produced without a precipitate forming.
  • Certain transitional metal ions have colors that can be recognized fairly easily (see table 1 above).
  • Only certain combinations of ions will form precipitates (see table 2).
  • Temperature change: A noticeable change in temperature, either an increase (exothermic reaction) or decrease (endothermic reaction), can indicate the occurrence of a chemical reaction.
  • New scent: The production of a new odor or the change in scent during a reaction can be an indication of a chemical change. Some reactions can produce characteristic or distinctive smells.
  • Formation of light or a flame: Some reactions involve the production of light or the presence of a flame, indicating the release of energy or the occurrence of a combustion reaction.

Procedure:

Part A: Observe solution mixtures for signs of a chemical reaction.

Using 6 total solutions, you will mix every combination of 2 of the 6 solutions and observe the mixture for signs of a chemical reaction such as color change, formation of a precipitate, a change in temperature of the solution, and bubble formation.
  1. Using your permanent marker, and labeling tape, label all the mixtures you will be making. If you have a test tube rack(s) with enough rows and columns, you may want to make a grid like table 4.
    • 1 & 2, 1 & 3, 1 & 4, 1 & 5, 1 & 6
    • 2 & 3, 2 & 4, 2 & 5, 2 & 6
    • 3 & 4, 3 & 5, 3 & 6
    • 4 & 5, 4 & 6
    • 5 & 6
  2. In 5 separate test tubes, mix 10-12 drops of solution 1 with 10-12 drops of each of the other 5 solutions (2-6) one at a time recording any observations (remember to feel the test tube to see if it becomes hot or cold). Record your observations in the white spaces in table 4.
    • 1 & 2, 1 & 3, 1 & 4, 1 & 5, 1 & 6
  3. In 4 separate test tubes, mix 10-12 drops of solution 2 with 10-12 drops of solutions 3 through 6 one at a time recording any observations (remember to feel the test tube to see if it becomes hot or cold). Record your observations in the white spaces in table 4.
    • 2 & 3, 2 & 4, 2 & 5, 2 & 6
  4. In 3 separate test tubes, mix 10-12 drops of solution 3 with 10-12 drops of solutions 4 through 6 one at a time recording any observations (remember to feel the test tube to see if it becomes hot or cold). Record your observations in the white spaces in table 4.
    • 3 & 4, 3 & 5, 3 & 6
  5. In 2 separate test tubes, mix 10-12 drops of solution 4 with 10-12 drops of solutions 5 through 6 one at a time recording any observations (remember to feel the test tube to see if it becomes hot or cold). Record your observations in the white spaces in table 4.
    • 4 & 5, 4 & 6
  6. In another test tube, mix 10-12 drops of solution 5 with 10-12 drops of solution 6 and record any observations (remember to feel the test tube to see if it becomes hot or cold). Record your observations in the white spaces in table 4
  7. Copy your observations to the slightly shaded cells as well so that you can more easily read the results for one solution by going across the chart. E.g. row 2 column 1 should say the same thing as row 1 column 2.

Part B: Identifying your solutions based on your observations.

Based on your observations, and the information in the background (including the tables mentioned in the background), you will identify the each of the six solutions from the following list: nickle(II) nitrate (Ni(NO3)2), sulfuric acid (H2SO4), silver nitrate (AgNO3), ammonium carbonate ((NH4)2CO3), potassium chloride (KCl), sodium hydroxide (NaOH).
  1. Identify each of the solutions from the list above and record them in table 5.
    • If you’re having trouble, start with the more obvious reactions such as when a non-white precipitate was formed, when a gas was formed, and when the reaction was exothermic or endothermic.
    • Next identify the rest based on whether they formed a precipitate or not using the solubility rules.

Part C: Identifying an unknown solution

You will mix an unknown solution ((NH4)2CO3, Ni(NO3)2, KCl, AgNO3, NaOH, or H2SO4) with 2 different solutions you’ve identified (AgNO3 & NaOH) to determine the identity of the unknown solution (you may need to add more trials to determine the identity of your unknown).
  1. Create a flow chart you can use to identify your unknown solution by combining it with AgNO3 & NaOH. Use figure 1 as an example.
    • Boxes with rounded corners should contain solution numbers
    • Diamond shaped boxes should contain the solution you are adding to your unknown (i.e. AgNO3 & NaOH)
    • Boxes with sharp corners should contain the possible outcomes of mixing your unknown solution with your known solution.
  2. In 3 separate test tubes, mix 10-12 drops of your unknown solution with 10-12 drops of AgNO3 & NaOH, and record your observations in table 6.
