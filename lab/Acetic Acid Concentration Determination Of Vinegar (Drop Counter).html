Acetic Acid Concentration Determination of Vinegar (Titration)

Name:

Chemicals and lab equipment:

  • Large waste beaker
  • Solid KHP (potassium hydrogen phthalate) (204.22 g/mol)
  • 5% acetic acid vinegar (~30 mL per group)
  • 250 mL beaker
  • 0.25 M NaOH solution (~150 mL per group)
  • 400 mL beaker
  • Evaporation dish or watch glass
  • Scale
  • Weighing boats
  • Scoopulas or spatulas

Each lab group:

  • LabQuest
  • pH probe
  • Drop counter, buret, and microstirrer
  • Ring stand & buret clamp
  • Phenopthalene
  • (2) 150 mL beakers
  • 50 mL graduated cylinder
  • 100 mL beaker
  • 10 mL volumetric pipet or graduate pipet
  • Pipet pump
  • Plastic (Pasteur) pipet
  • White piece of paper
  • Rubber stopper for flask
  • Stir plate
  • Stir bar
  • 400 or 600 mL waste beaker
  • Permanent marker
  • Wash bottle with deionized water

Equations and calculations:

Molarity (M) = $\frac{mol_{solute}}{L_{solution}}$
Molar mass = $\frac{grams}{mol}$
For aA → bB, Mole A x $\frac{b\;mol\;B}{a\;mol\;A}$ = Mol B

Safety:

  • NaOH is corrosive, and can cause chemical burns to the skin, eyes, and respiratory system.
  • Acetic acid (HC2H3O2) is an irritant to the eyes, skin, and respiratory system.
  • KHP (potassium hydrogen phthalate) is an eye and skin irritant, and can cause damage to the respiratory system.
  • Wear safety goggles or glasses at all times.
  • Wear nitrile gloves at all times.

Procedure:

Part A: Setting up your titration apparatus.

You will set up your titration apparatus and rinse your buret with water and 15 mL of NaOH.
  1. Set up your titration apparatus (see figure 1).
    Figure 1: Titration apparatus

  2. Rinse your buret by filling your buret with deionized water, then let all of it run into your waste beaker. Use this opportunity to also calibrate your drop counter.
    3. Steps for calibrating a drop counter
    1. Set up your drop counter, and buret so that the buret will drop droplets through the photogate on the drop counter and into a waste beaker. The top stopcock is used to turn the buret on and off, and the bottom one is used to control the rate of the drops. Take this opportunity to establish a drop rate of about 1 drop per second.
    2. On the LabQuest press Sensors => Calibrate => GDX: Drop Counter.
    3. Position an empty 10 mL graduated cylindar below the buret and drop counter to catch the drops.
    4. When you are ready press Calibrate Now, dispense 9 mL of fluid, and stop the drops by turning the top stopcock.
    5. Read the volume off of the graduated cylindar (to 0.1 mL), and put it into the LabQuest.
    6. Press OK
  3. Obtain approximately 150 mL of NaOH in a 250 mL flask and bring it back to your lab bench. Make sure to keep this solution stoppered when not in use.
  4. Pour about 60 mL of the NaOH solution from your flask into your 100 mL beaker.
  5. From your 100 mL beaker, fill the buret with NaOH solution (with the bottom stop cock horizontal/closed).
  6. Drain the NaOH (a small amount may remain at the bottom of the buret), then refill the buret with about 60 mL.
  7. Record the beginning volume of the NaOH solution in table 1 (note: Assuming you reset your labquest, this should be 0.00 mL, not your estimate of how much NaOH is in the buret).

    8. Part A: Standardization of a NaOH solution with KHP.

  8. Because NaOH solutions are basic, they will absorb carbon dioxide from the air, which will neutralize the base over time. For this reason, it is important to standardize the concentration of NaOH at the beginning of your experiment. Since all groups will be using the same NaOH solution, you will average your results to get a more accurate molarity. To standardize the NaOH solution you will measure out a mass of solid KHP on a scale, because that gives a very accurate and precise measurement.
  9. Measure out 1.000 g to 1.200 g of KHP in a weighing boat. Remember to tare the scale after placing the weighing boat on it so that you’re only measuring the mass of the KHP. Record your actual mass in table 1.
  10. Measure out 50 mL of deionized water with the 50 mL graduated cylinder.
  11. Dissolve your sample of KHP in 50 mL of deionized water in a 150 mL beaker.
  12. Add 2-3 drops of phenolphthalein to the solution.
  13. Make sure your pH meter is submursed in the beaker (not just the microstirrer).
  14. Calculate the estimated amount of approximately 0.25 M NaOH needed to neutralize the amount of KHP you measured.
    15. g KHP x $\frac{1\;mol\;KHP}{204.22\;g\;KHP}$ x $\frac{1\;mol\;NaOH}{1\;mol\;KHP}$ x $\frac{1\;L\;NaOH}{0.25\;mol\;NaOH}$ x $\frac{1000\;mL}{1\;L}$ =
    mL NaOH
  15. Record this volume in table 1.
  16. Turn the stirring plate on to a rotation slow enough to prevent splashing.
  17. Press the "play button" on the labquest to begin recording data, and turn the bottom stopcock to start your titration.
  18. One you start to see puffs of pink in the solution, make sure that you have slowed the buret down to about 1 drop per second (or slower).
  19. When the puffs of pink reach about 50% of the volume of the solution before the color goes away, you are likely within a few drops of the end point, and you may want to use the bottom stopcock to only allow one drop at a time.
  20. You can use a few drops from your wash bottle to wash a drop sticking to the bottom of the buret if you think that might be the last drop needed.
  21. Your solution should be the lightest color pink you can achieve with the solution remaining pink for at least 30 seconds.
  22. Once you reach the end point, record the final volume (the volume you read off the buret, which should be close to the estimated required volume, not the volume you estimate is in the buret) in table 1, and calculate the volume of NaOH solution you used.
  23. Pour the slightly pink solution down the drain with running water.

    27. Part A: Determination of acetic acid (HC2H3O2) concentration in vinegar.
  27. Refill the buret as close to the 0.0 mL mark as possible.
  28. Record the volume of the NaOH solution to 2 decimal places in table 3.
  29. Obtain 10 mL of vinegar using a 10 mL volumetric or graduated pipet and put it in another 150 mL beaker. Record the actual mL of vinegar below.
    30. mL Vinegar

  31. Turn the stirring plate on to a rotation slow enough to prevent splashing.
  32. One you start to see puffs of pink in the solution, make sure that you have slowed the buret down to about 1 drop per second (or slower).

  34. You can use a few drops from your wash bottle to wash a drop sticking to the bottom of the buret if you think that might be the last drop needed.
  35. Your solution should be the lightest color pink you can achieve with the solution remaining pink for at least 30 seconds.
  36. Once you reach the end point, record the final volume in table 3, and calculate the volume of NaOH solution you used.

  40. Collect all your waste in your 400 mL waste beaker. If the solution appears light pink, then you can pour it down the drain with the water running, if it turns clear again, then slowly add more NaOH until it turns light pink, then dispose of it down the drain.
