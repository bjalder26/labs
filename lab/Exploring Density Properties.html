Exploring Density Properties Lab

Name:

Complete the "Density - Density of a Plastic Cube" virtual lab in ALEKS. The purpose of this lab is to become acquainted with laboratory practices while exploring density, identifying unknown solids/liquids, and verifying the percent composition of a penny based on density. Be certain to follow all instructions. Always properly dispose of broken glass and chemical waste according to your instructor.

Information on US Coins:

Materials:

  • Seawater
  • Unknown liquid
  • Unknown solid
  • Pennies minted after 1983
  • 50 mL graduated cylinder (3)
  • 100 mL beaker
  • Ruler/Caliper
  • Distilled or deionized water
  • Balance

Equations and Calculations:

  • Density = $\frac{mass}{volume}$ ($\frac{g}{mL}$)
  • Metric conversions
  • Volume = length x width x height (cm3)
  • Volume of a cylinder = pi x radius2 x height (cm3)
  • Percent composition of a penny: x (copper density) + (1 - x) (zinc density) = average density
    • Where x = the gram fraction of copper in the penny (e.g. 20% copper would be 0.20)
    • 1-x = gram fraction of zing
    • Gram fraction x 100 = percent by mass
  • percent error = $\frac{|measured\;value\;-\;known\;value|}{known\;value}$ x 100%

Safety:

  • Goggles must be worn at all times.
  • Appropriate clothing must be worn at all times.

Procedure:

Part A: Density of Water

  1. Place a 50 mL graduated cylindar on the scale and record the mass of the graduated cylindar.
  2. Measure 50 mL of distilled water, and record your actual volume to the nearest estimated 0.1 mL.
  3. Weigh the mass of the 50 mL graduated cylindar and water. Record the mass in the table below.
  4. Calculate the density of the distilled water (formula shown above).
  5. Repeat steps 1-4 for the second trial.
  6. Calculate the average density from your first and second trials.
  7. Record the lab temperature in table 1.
  8. Using the Density of Water at Different Temperatures table, calcuate the percent error for your average density and record it in table 1.

Part D: Density of an Unknown Solid

  1. Select an unknown metal from the samples provided.
  2. Weigh the metal and record the mass.
  3. Use a ruler (or caliper) to measure the volume; (measure length, width, and height for a cube, or diameter and height for a cylinder - use the volume formula above to calculate volume). Record the volume.
    • Note: you may need to convert from mm to cm
  4. Calculate the density using the ruler (or caliper) method for volume and the displacement method for volume.
  5. Use a graduated cylinder to determine volume by water displacement. Record the volume to the nearest estimated 0.1 mL.
  6. Repeat steps 2-5 for the second trial.
  7. Take an average of the densities by water displacement for your two trials.
  8. Compare your average density to the densities in table 7 to determine the identity of your unknown solid.

Part E: Density of a Penny

  1. Collect 26 pennies from the pool provided.
  2. Weigh the pennies and record the mass.
  3. Fill a 50 mL graduated cylinder to 40.0 mL, then add the 26 pennies one-at-a-time
  4. Roll the pennies into the graduated cylinder to minimize splashing and bubbles getting trapped under the pennies.
  5. Periodically tap the side of the graduated cylinder to loosen up any trapped bubbles (bubbles will alter your volume measurement).
  6. Determine volume by water displacement (subtract the ending volume from the beginning volume of 40.0 mL). Record the volume to the nearest estimated 0.1 mL.
  7. Calculate the density of the pennies.
  8. Completely dry your pennies, then repeat steps 2-7 for trial 2.
  9. Calculate the average density from your two trials.
  10. Calculate percent composition of copper and zinc (using the formula at the beginning).
