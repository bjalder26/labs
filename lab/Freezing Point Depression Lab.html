Background

A colligative property is when a solute affects a solution property like its freezing or boiling point, and the effect depends only on the amount of particles dissolved, no matter which substance you use. In this lab we will investigate the effect of the different solutes on the freezing point of aqueous solutions.

You should observe that as the solute concentration increases the freezing point Tf(solution) decreases (the freezing point depression ΔT increases), and this can be described by the following equation:
$\huge{ΔT = i⋅k_f⋅\frac{n_{solute}}{m_{solvent}}}$
$\large{where}$
$\large{i=Van't\;Hoff\;Factor}$
$\large{k_f=freezing\;point\;depression\;constant}$
$\large{n=moles\;solute}$
$\large{m=Kg\;solvent}$
$\large{and}$
$\large{ΔT=T_f(pure\;solvent)−T_f(solution)}$
The value of kf for the solvent water is 1860 °C g/mol, or 1.86 °C kg/mol. Check your units to decide which format is more convenient in this case.

Experimental Design Considerations

We are making the assumption that at the instance the ice melts the mixture of ice and water is at the freezing point. There can be multiple reasons why this is wrong, and we will discuss this in our pre-lab discussion.

Part 1: Freezing point determination, pure water

  1. Obtain a temperature probe and turn it on.
  2. Pour approximately 25 mL of DI water into the 100 mL beaker
  3. Place temperature probe into pure water in beaker
  4. Add around 20 mL of fresh crushed ice to a 50 mL beaker.
  5. Quickly add the ice to the 100 mL beaker, gently swirl the temperature probe. Record the lowest temperature, and the temperature just as the last bit of ice melts.
  6. Weigh the mass of the beaker with the water and melted ice (so now just water). Record to the precision of the instrument. Clean and dry the 100 mL beaker.
  7. Discard the water in the sink.
  8. In your further calculations, use the lowest recorded temperature as the freezing point of water. The temperature probes are not calibrated, but are very good at measuring temperature differences. Don't be worried if the reading on your temperature probe is different from that of your neighbor, and don't swap temperature probes.

    Background:

    Magnesium oxide is an ionic compound composed of magnesium (Mg) and oxygen (O) atoms. The empirical formula represents the simplest whole-number ratio of elements in a compound. In this experiment, students will determine the empirical formula of magnesium oxide by burning magnesium in the presence of oxygen.

    When magnesium is heated and exposed to oxygen, it undergoes a chemical reaction known as combustion. The magnesium reacts with oxygen to form magnesium oxide. The product will have a larger mass than the magnesium because oxygen will bond to the magnesium producing magnesium oxide.

    ? Mg + ? O2 → ? Mg?O?

    By measuring the masses of magnesium and magnesium oxide, students can determine the empirical formula of magnesium oxide.

    Safety:

    • Handle the crucible and heated materials with tongs or crucible tongs to avoid burns.
    • Wear safety goggles or glasses and lab gloves at all times.
    • Do not look at the magnesium as it is burning.

    Procedure:

    Part A: Prepare to burn magnesium.

    You will measure out your magnesium and set up your Bunsen burner, ring stand, iron ring, clay triangle, and crucible.
    1. Inspect your crucible for cracks. If it has cracks, then discard it and get another crucible.
    2. Rinse your crucible and lid with deionized water, and dry it off with paper towels. You will probably not be able to get the crucible completely clean, nor is that your goal.
    3. Attach an iron ring to a ring stand, place a clay triangle on the ring, then place your crucible and lid on the clay triangle (see figure 1).
    4. Check the Bunsen burner hose for cracks, and get another hose if yours has cracks.
    5. Attach the Bunsen burner to the gas line and the Bunsen burner.
    6. Turn on the gas and light your Bunsen burner.
    7. Adjust the air vent on your Bunsen burner to produce a blue flame with an inner blue cone. The tip of this inner blue cone is the hottest part of the flame (see figure 1).
    8. Being careful not to burn yourself, adjust your iron ring to place the crucible right on top of the inner blue cone and use the crucible tongs to tilt the lid (see figure 1).
    9. Heat the crucible for a minimum of 5 minutes (if you can still see smoke, then heat it longer).
    10. Turn off the gas to the Bunsen burner and use crucible tongs to remove the crucible and place it on a ceramic tile (if available).
    11. Allow the crucible to cool for 15 minutes, then weigh it and record the mass in table 1.
    Figure 1. Bunsen burner flame and crucible over flame

    Part B: Burning the magnesium.

    You will carefully burn a magnesium strip, and make sure it has burned completely.
    1. Weigh out approximately 0.3 grams of magnesium ribbon and record the actual mass in table 1.
    2. Fold up the magnesium ribbon and place it in the bottom of the crucible. Then place the crucible back on the clay triangle and begin heating the crucible again with the lid tilted (see figure 1).
    3. Make sure the bottom of the crucible is right on top of the tip of the inner blue cone of the flame - otherwise it may not get hot enough to burn.
    4. If the magnesium begins to burn brightly, then do not look at the light, and cover the crucible for a few minutes releasing the smoke periodically. You do not want to let the smoke escape.
    5. When it seems like no more smoke is being given off, then cover the crucible for a few minutes and see if any smoke comes out when you uncover it again. More smoke would indicate that it is not done burning.
    6. The product should also appear as a white powdery substance.
    7. Heat the crucible for an additional 5 minutes with the cover completely off after it appears to not smoke anymore. The product should appear white.
    8. Turn off the gas, and remove the crucible from the clay triangle with the crucible tongs.
    9. Place it on a ceramic tile (if available) to cool for 15 minutes.

    Part C: Removing nitrogen.

    Since nitrogen is the major component of air, some nitrogen oxides will be formed. You will wet the magnesium oxide product and heat it again to remove the nitrogen.
    1. Using a plastic (Pasteur) pipet, add about 6-8 drops of water to your magnesium oxide. This will react with the nitrogen producing ammonia gas.
    2. Heat the crucible over the Bunsen burner again with the lid removed for another 5 minutes.
    3. Turn off the gas, and remove the crucible from the clay triangle with the crucible tongs.
    4. Place it on a ceramic tile (if available) to cool for 15 minutes.
    5. Do not try to remove your magnesium oxide product at this point.
    6. Measure the mass of the magnesium oxide, crucible and lid together, and record the mass in table 1.
    7. Calculate the mass of the magnesium oxide product (mass of crucible, lid, and magnesium oxide minus mass of crucible and lid) and write it in table 1.
    8. Calculate the mass of oxygen in the magnesium oxide (magnesium oxide mass minus magnesium mass) and write it in table 1.
