Background:

In part A of the lab, you will be performing a activity series experiments to investigate the reactivity of metals with water and hydrochloric acid. The activity series is a list of metals arranged in order of their reactivity, with the most reactive metal at the top and the least reactive at the bottom. Highly reactive metals will react vigorously with room temperature water. Less reactive metals may require concentrated acid to react, or may not react at all. By observing the reactions of different metals with water and acid, you will be able to determine their relative positions in the activity series.
Litmus paper is an indicator that changes color in the presence of acids or bases. You will also test whether the water becomes more basic when you add metals. By noting the color change, you can assess the acidity or basicity of the water. Red litmus paper will turn blue in the presence of a basic solution. In part B of the lab, you will be investigating the solubility of alkaline earth metals (Group II metals) by observing the formation of precipitates when their chloride salts are mixed with various reagents. The solubility of these salts will provide insights into the trend of solubility among the alkaline Earth metals. Chloride salts of magnesium, calcium, strontium, and barium are typically soluble in water. You will attempt to react them with ammonium carbonate, sodium sulfate, and potassium iodate, and will look for a precipitate (solid) to form to indicate a reaction occurred. Precipitation of an insoluble compound indicates low solubility, while no precipitation suggests higher solubility. By observing the formation or absence of precipitates in the wells and referring to Figure 2, you will determine the solubility of the alkaline earth metal salts and analyze the trend in solubility among the group.

Safety:

  • Proper Disposal: Dispose of used litmus paper, metals, and solutions according to your institution's guidelines for chemical waste disposal.
  • Caution with Temperature: Be cautious when handling the wells containing solutions, as hydrochloric acid can cause temperature changes. Use a thermometer to measure the temperature of the wells before touching them.
  • Good Ventilation: Ensure you are working in a well-ventilated area or under a fume hood to prevent the build-up of any potentially harmful gases.
  • Prevent Cross-Contamination: Use separate pipets for different solutions to avoid cross-contamination and ensure accurate results.
  • Wear safety goggles or glasses at all times.
  • Wear nitrile gloves at all times.

Part A: Activity Series

  1. Place your 24 well plate on top of a white piece of paper.
  2. Using a plastic (Pasteur) pipet, put 20 drops of deionized water in wells D1-D3.
  3. Using forceps, test the water in wells D1-D3 with red litmus paper, and write the color of the litmus paper in table 1.
  4. Using a different plastic (Pasteur) pipet (or a dropper bottle), put 20 drops of 0.5 M HCl in wells D4-D6.
  5. Using forceps, add a small ball (~1 cm) of aluminum foil to well D1, and observe it for any reaction. Record your observations in table 1.
  6. Using forceps, add a small ball (~1 cm) of aluminum foil to well D4, and observe it for any reaction. Record your observations in table 1.
  7. Using forceps, add a piece of magnesium turnings to well D2, and observe it for any reaction. Record your observations in table 1.
  8. Using forceps, add a piece of magnesium turnings to well D5, and observe it for any reaction. Record your observations in table 1.
  9. Using forceps, add one piece of calcium turnings to well D3, and observe it for any reaction. Record your observations in table 1.
  10. Using forceps, add one piece of calcium turnings to well D6, and observe it for any reaction. Record your observations in table 1.
  11. Using a thermometer, check the temperature of wells D4-D6, and record their temperature in table D1.
  12. Using forceps, retest the water in wells D1-D3 with red litmus paper, and write the color of the litmus paper in table 1.
  13. Recheck wells D1-D6, for bubble formation. Some of the reactions can occur slowly. Record your observations in table 1, and write NR for “no reaction” if there is no evidence of a reaction.
